## The Ones Who Benefit Most Are Sounding the Alarm

Sam Altman of OpenAI ran a basic income experiment for several years, directly handing $1,000 a month to 1,000 adults across Texas and Illinois, and has since wrapped it up. Bill Gates, founder of Microsoft, has argued that if a robot does the same job as a person, that robot should be taxed like one. Pope Leo XIV, in his encyclical _Magnificat Humanitas_ issued shortly after taking office, addressed head-on the economic inequality and erosion of human dignity brought on by AI.

What deserves attention is where these three stand. None of them need to worry about AI-driven inequality. If anything, they belong to the class that stands to gain the most from it. The fact that they are the ones raising the alarm first suggests this problem has already arrived — not as a future risk, but as a present condition that crosses ideological and class lines.

## Basic Income May Be an Anesthetic, Not a Cure

[^1]Research by MIT economists Daron Acemoglu and Pascual Restrepo found that each additional robot eliminated roughly 3.3 workers, and that adding one robot per thousand workers reduced wages by 0.42 percent. The proposed responses — universal basic income and taxation on AI profits — are not unreasonable in their direction. But the prescription carries a trap that is easy to miss.

History offers a comparable experiment. [^2]Nigeria rode an oil boom in the 1970s, but as the country grew dependent on petroleum revenues, its cocoa and groundnut export industries collapsed. When oil came to account for 70 to 80 percent of government income, there was little incentive left to tax, invest in manufacturing, or diversify. When global oil prices fell in the 1980s, GDP per capita dropped by more than 40 percent. Venezuela followed a similar path. The Chávez government channeled oil revenues into large-scale welfare programs, and poverty rates fell in the short term. When prices collapsed, an economy that had lost the habit of producing for itself could not recover. Economists call this Dutch Disease: unearned income flowing in from the outside gradually hollows out the productive instincts of an economy. The structural logic of AI-funded basic income is not so different.

Altman's own experiment pointed in the same direction. Recipients still wanted to work. Those who were unemployed reported guilt and frustration. Even when survival was no longer the immediate concern, the motivation to contribute did not fill the gap on its own. This is what behavioral economists call the crowding-out effect — external rewards displacing internal drive. Money resolves the material problem. It does not resolve the human one.

## Who Controls the Terms of Distribution

The more fundamental question is who gets to set the terms. Under the current structure, AI companies decide how much they earn and how much they share. That is not a right extended to the public — it is charity. If a company changes course, lobbies its way around taxation, or simply earns less, the distribution stops. The logical response is for the state to take over that authority: democratic governments controlling taxation and redistribution. That direction makes sense. But it raises its own unresolved question. By what standard does a government distribute? To whom, by how much, and on what basis? This is not a problem AI has created. It is a problem politics has been unable to solve for centuries.

And when distributed money flows into the market, prices rise. The real value of basic income erodes year by year. People will demand increases and take to the streets when they do not come. Thomas Piketty argued in _Capital in the Twenty-First Century_ that as long as the return on capital exceeds the rate of economic growth, wealth will concentrate among those who already hold capital — not as an exception, but as the default logic of the system. AI accelerates that logic. After the First Industrial Revolution, it took decades for workers' real wages to catch up with the productivity gains. There is no evidence yet that the lag will be shorter this time.

## The Individual Choice While Waiting Out the Gap

How individuals navigate that gap is, ultimately, the question that matters most. When the printing press was invented, books and knowledge belonged to the clergy and the nobility. Over time, anyone could become an author. AI is following the same trajectory. The tools are getting cheaper and more accessible. Solo entrepreneurs using AI are already matching the output of teams ten times their size. Just as most people today hold a job of some kind, a world where most people run some kind of small operation of their own may not be far off. AI as the tool; the individual as the designer and seller of their own value.

Systemic redistribution remains necessary. But for those who do not want to end up at the back of the line waiting for it, the more practical position is on the side that produces rather than the side that receives. That is the most realistic preparation available to any individual right now.
